A Losing Battle is the 11th story of Chapter 2, Volume 1 of the main story.

Descriptions:

  • The Kaiser board member reveals their ambitions to find a massive treasure buried somewhere in Abydos. Then, using the Foreclosure Task Force's debt as leverage, the creditor forces them to back down. Hoshino calls for a retreat to the chagrin of the other task force members.

Story Parts

1. Ako's Self-Criticism Paper

  • Ako talked big and claimed that she could write 1000 pages, but didn't expect Hina would actually be doing so. During the break, Ako asks Hina whether she knew about Hoshino. Hina says it's the first time for her to meet her, and discloses that she's listed as a potential threat by Gehenna's Intelligence Agency for her ability; but she also agreed with Ako that Hoshino does seem to be completely different in these two years. Hina also mentions about the battle at then, believing that if Ako continued combating, they could sustain a great loss because of Schale's assistance. She criticizes Ako for being too impulsive.

3. Kaiser Director

  • Kaiser Director introduces himself to everyone, which is immediately got under fire. But the director doesn't care, and says that given the current situation, it is true that the Foreclosure Task Force has invaded their own private lands. He also says that the troops are all for searching treasures beneath Abydos, not for invading Abydos High School. Nobody is convinced, and director demonstrates just how easy it would be for him to "actually sabotage Abydos".

4. A Debt Can't Be Filled

  • Kaiser Director makes a call and says that Abydos High School's credit is at all-time low, and the interest rate will be raised 30 times higher. At the club room, Ayane answers the call and is greatly shocked. Kaiser Director also says that they would first need to pay a deposit in advance to prove their repayment ability. But that could never be achieved - and so, the director advises them to give up or drop out the school, or simply transfer to another one. No one is willing to step a step back. Hoshino says that it's useless to argue here and suggests to leave first.
